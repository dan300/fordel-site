How was the study conducted?

We have identified the 106 wedding planners we put into our study at the end of August, 2016. We searched Google, looked at roundup posts, industry directories and the UKAWP directory. Our list is probably not totally extensive, meaning there might be wedding planners left out, but these planners are definitely some of the most visible ones if someone is using online tools. We started following them and subscribing to their email autoresponders (you’ll see why we did that soon) and did an initial data collection. This consisted 47 data points, including but not limited to the following:

  • Amount of tracking codes in their code
  • Do they have Facebook, Twitter, Google+, Instagram and Pinterest accounts?
  • If so, how many followers they have?
  • How engaged people are with their latest posts?
  • Do they have a blog?
  • Do they collect email addresses?
  • What call to actions they use in their opt-in forms?
  • Do they have photo and/or video galleries?
We analyzed the collected data and drew some initial conclusions that were not meant to be final, we just wanted to get an initial understanding of the market. Then, in mid December, 2016 we collected all the data again and analyzed it once more. The main insights remained, with only slight differences. So there was no need to rewrite our conclusions. We decided that we take out two wedding planners from the social media related data set because they were just so big that they skewed the findings. The reason for this was that they had a lot more (8-9 times more) Facebook page likes than anyone else but they were not very active on Facebook. This following size was definitely not achieved through social media though, rather it is just a result of their already existing brand recognition. Both of these companies are not only UK based and they do more general event planning as well, they were featured on major news outlets like CNN and they work with big global luxury brands as well as celebrities.

Appendix

Notes on measurement

Google Analytics obviously is not the only measurement tool you can use but it’s the most widely adopted especially among small and middle sized businesses. This is the reason why we specifically looked for Google Analytics codes in wedding planners’ sites and not other measurement tools. But we also checked whether they have any measurement in their code and most of those that don’t have Google Analytics don’t have any other measurement either, so it’s safe to say that the case is that those that don’t use Google Analytics probably don’t even track anything. There are analytics solutions (server side measurement) that don’t require the site owner to install any tracking code to their site, but our assumption based on our experience is that in practice there are no such small businesses that only use server side measurement to measure their marketing efforts.

Why should blogs be hosted on the primary domain?

We are not saying you should do a blog only to get SEO results. Having a blog can be a significant investment though, so what we definitely say is that you should get the most out of that investment. If you put in resources to build up and maintain your blog and you do it on your own domain (i.e. companyname.co.uk/blog or at least blog.companyname.co.uk, instead of companyname-blog-com or companynameblog.blogspot.com) all that useful content will be indexed by search engines and attributed to your site. This increases the authority of your domain, which helps your site rank to other search terms as well. You can put a link to your blog from your main site and vice versa but that’s not the same as having the best of your content increasing the authority of the site on which you have all the information available of your business.
Simply having a blog of course is not the same as having a great marketing activity. We have found that only 23% of the wedding planner blogs have been updated within a week from the date we checked them and 56% were updated over a month ago. We are not saying that you should prefer quantity over quality and churn out 10 posts each week just for the sake of having more content and look more active. We say though that having frequent (2-4 post / week) content updates which is based on a well defined strategy and on a good understanding of who you are talking to significantly shortens your the time it takes for you to learn both about your audience and about what works for you.
